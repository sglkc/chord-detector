\abstract{Automatic chord recognition (ACR) is a fundamental task in Music Information Retrieval (MIR) with broad applications in music education, interactive performance feedback, and real-time transcription systems. This paper presents the design, training, cross-validation, and evaluation of a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) model for 36-class piano chord classification using Constant-Q Transform (CQT) spectrograms as input features, and compares it against a hyperparameter-tuned Support Vector Machine (SVM) with a Radial Basis Function (RBF) kernel. The training dataset consists of 7,200 audio samples of 36 piano chord classes, spanning major, minor, and diminished triads across 12 chromatic roots in the fourth octave, recorded at 48 kHz from a Techno T-9890i keyboard. Generalization performance is evaluated on a separate 1,440-sample evaluation dataset recorded from a different instrument (Yamaha PSR-S910 keyboard) in a different environment, with no overlap with training data. The CNN architecture comprises four sequential Conv2D and MaxPooling2D blocks with filter sizes escalating from 64 to 256, followed by a fully connected dense layer with dropout regularization, trained using the Adam optimizer with early stopping. Both models achieve near-perfect accuracy on cross-validation (CNN: 1.0000, SVM: 1.0000 across five folds). However, cross-dataset evaluation reveals a decisive performance gap: the CNN retains high per-class accuracy across the majority of chord classes, with misclassifications concentrated in harmonically related pairs, while the SVM collapses entirely, misclassifying the overwhelming majority of samples from all classes as C\_diminished\_4, resulting in worse accuracy. These results demonstrate that CNN's hierarchical spectral feature learning generalizes substantially better than the RBF SVM's when acoustic conditions shift between training and evaluation environments, confirming CNN as the superior architecture for practical deployment in piano chord recognition systems.}

\section{Introduction}\label{sec1}

Music, as a universal art form, communicates emotion and structure through its constituent harmonic elements, of which the chord is among the most fundamental \cite{Adegbija2023}\cite{Ito2023}\cite{Putra2025}. A chord, defined as the simultaneous or near-simultaneous sounding of three or more distinct pitches, forms the harmonic backbone of Western tonal music. Identifying chords from raw audio signals, known as Automatic Chord Recognition (ACR), has significant practical applications including music transcription, score following, interactive music education, and real-time performance assistance \cite{Akram2025}\cite{Bortolozzo2021}\cite{Majchrzak2025}. Manual chord transcription is time-consuming and demands substantial ear-training and music theory expertise, creating a clear motivation for automated solutions \cite{Saputra2021}\cite{Telila2023}\cite{Majchrzak2025}.

ACR is a central problem within Music Information Retrieval (MIR), an interdisciplinary field that combines signal processing, machine learning, and music cognition to extract semantically meaningful information from audio recordings \cite{Majchrzak2025} \cite{Putra2025}. In ACR systems, the general approach involves segmenting an audio signal into short temporal windows, extracting a spectral feature representation from each window, and classifying each segment according to a predefined chord vocabulary \cite{Bortolozzo2021} \cite{Majchrzak2025}. The quality of this pipeline depends critically on two factors: the discriminative power of the feature representation and the generalization capacity of the classifier. Particularly when the classifier must operate on audio recorded from instruments or environments that differ from those in the training data.

Among the various time-frequency representations available for audio feature extraction, the Constant-Q Transform (CQT) is particularly well-suited for music analysis \cite{Li2021} \cite{Li2021}. Unlike the Short-Time Fourier Transform (STFT), which distributes frequency bins linearly, CQT bins are spaced logarithmically, directly aligning each bin with the frequency of a musical semitone \cite{Li2021} \cite{Lacoste2007}. This alignment means that the harmonic structure of a chord which comprising a root note, a third, and a fifth, manifests as a consistent and recognizable pattern of activated frequency bins regardless of the chord's root pitch, making CQT features naturally suited to CNN-based pattern recognition and providing robustness to the different instrument tonal variations inherent in cross-dataset evaluation \cite{Li2021} \cite{Telila2023}. Empirical comparisons confirm that CQT features outperform STFT and CENS chroma features when used as input to CNN classifiers, with CQT achieving 84.2\% accuracy compared to 75.3\% for STFT alone in a standard ACR benchmark \cite{Li2021}.

Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) have emerged as the dominant architecture for spectrogram-based audio classification due to their ability to learn hierarchical, locally-invariant feature representations \cite{OShea2015} \cite{Moysis2023} \cite{Zaman2023}. When applied to CQT spectrograms, CNN convolutional kernels learn to identify the characteristic frequency patterns (harmonic overtones and spectral envelope shapes) that distinguish one chord type from another, operating on small local receptive fields rather than requiring the full global feature vector \cite{Akram2025} \cite{Purwono2022}. Multiple studies have demonstrated the effectiveness of the CNN-CQT combination for chord recognition and music transcription tasks across diverse datasets \cite{Li2021} \cite{Telila2023} \cite{Ferdiawan2022} \cite{Li2021} \cite{Ito2023}. Critically, the local spatial nature of CNN feature learning, that is processing small neighborhoods in the time-frequency plane, is expected to provide greater robustness to global acoustic shifts (such as timbre changes between instruments or background noise) compared to classifiers that rely on global feature statistics.

Support Vector Machines (SVM) with RBF kernels represent the established state-of-the-art among conventional machine learning approaches for audio classification and have been applied to chord recognition tasks in numerous studies \cite{Cui2022} \cite{Putra2025}. SVMs seek a maximum-margin hyperplane in a high-dimensional feature space and are particularly effective when a clear class boundary exists in the training distribution \cite{Zaman2023}. However, SVM decision boundaries are defined in terms of the global structure of the training feature space: when the test distribution shifts due to changes in instrument timbre, recording environment, or microphone characteristics, the kernel similarity between test vectors and training support vectors may change substantially, causing the learned boundary to become unreliable. This susceptibility to distribution shift is a known limitation of kernel methods \cite{Zaman2023} \cite{Zaman2023} and is directly tested in the cross-dataset evaluation presented in this paper.

Despite the extensive ACR literature, several gaps remain. The majority of published systems focus on offline, large-vocabulary chord recognition using complex architectures, including hybrid CNN-HMM models \cite{Li2021}, CNN-RNN hybrids \cite{OHanlon2021}, conformer-based architectures \cite{Akram2025}, and transformer-attention mechanisms \cite{Akram2025} \cite{Kusaka2024}. These systems are evaluated on test sets or standardized benchmarks that do not test generalization across different datasets. The robustness of classifiers to acoustic domain shift, which arises from differences in instrument brand, piano voice preset, recording device, and room acoustics, has received limited systematic attention. This gap is particularly relevant for practical deployment, where users will interact with classification systems using their own instruments and recording setups, which inevitably differ from those used during model training.

This paper addresses these gaps by presenting a focused study of CNN model design, training, cross-validation, and cross-dataset evaluation for 36-class piano chord classification from CQT spectrograms, with a comparative evaluation against a hyperparameter-tuned RBF-SVM on the same feature set. The experimental design explicitly tests generalization under domain shift: the training dataset is recorded on one keyboard (Techno T-9890i, Acoustic Grand Piano voice, Xiaomi Redmi Pad 2 microphone) while the evaluation dataset is recorded on a different instrument (Yamaha PSR-S910, Grand Piano voice, ROG Flow X13 internal microphone) in a different physical environment, with all other conditions held constant. This setup enables a rigorous assessment of whether each model's learned representation generalizes beyond its training domain.

The specific contributions of this paper are:

\begin{enumerate}[1.]
\item A complete description and empirical validation of a four-block CNN architecture for 36-class piano chord classification using CQT spectrograms, achieving near-perfect accuracy (1.0000) under within-distribution cross-validation across five stratified folds.

\item A custom training dataset of 7,200 labeled piano chord audio samples (36 classes, 200 samples per class) and a separate 1,440 sample evaluation dataset recorded on a different instrument in a different acoustic environment, constituting a controlled cross-dataset generalization benchmark.

\item A rigorous comparative evaluation of CNN against a hyperparameter-tuned RBF-SVM under identical experimental conditions, demonstrating parity under within-distribution evaluation but a decisive divergence under cross-dataset testing — the CNN maintains meaningful per-class accuracy while the SVM collapses to degenerate single-class prediction.

\item A detailed analysis of the CNN's cross-dataset misclassification patterns, identifying the harmonically related chord pairs that constitute the principal sources of error and providing interpretable guidance for future data augmentation and fine-tuning strategies.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Works}\label{sec2}

The problem of automatic chord recognition has been approached with an evolving range of machine learning methodologies, with the dominant trend shifting progressively from hand-crafted chroma features towards end-to-end deep learning systems.

Li \cite{Li2021} investigated a CNN-HMM hybrid approach for audio-based musical chord recognition, comparing three feature types (STFT, CQT, and CENS) as inputs to CNN and DNN classifiers. The study found that CQT features yielded the highest individual accuracy of 84.2\% on the CNN model, compared to 75.3\% for STFT and 80.8\% for CENS, and that combining all three features further boosted performance to 86.5\%. This work established CQT as the preferred spectral feature for CNN-based chord classification and motivates its adoption in the present study.

O'Hanlon and Sandler \cite{OHanlon2021} proposed FifthNet, a compact CNN architecture incorporating a modified CQT representation that achieves performance comparable to larger state-of-the-art models while using substantially fewer parameters. The architecture demonstrated that computationally efficient CNNs can match complex models for chord recognition, providing practical guidance for designing lightweight classifiers. Ito et al. \cite{Ito2023} subsequently modified FifthNet to remove non-causal look-ahead frame dependencies, adapting the architecture for online inference, and observed a 1-2\% improvement in accuracy as an unexpected consequence of removing the look-ahead bias.

Telila et al. \cite{Telila2023} applied a CNN-CQT system to polyphonic piano automatic music transcription (AMT), achieving frame-level accuracy of 88.2\% (92.5\% excluding silent frames) on a MIDI-synthesized dataset. The study noted that low-energy silent frames substantially degrade measured accuracy and recommended their exclusion from training and evaluation. Moysis et al. \cite{Moysis2023} conducted a comprehensive survey of deep learning methods for music signal processing covering over 150 publications from 2016-2022, identifying CNN as a dominant architecture for chord recognition and confirming that spectrogram-based CNN models routinely achieve higher accuracy than other feature representations.

Akram et al. \cite{Akram2025} introduced ChordFormer, a state-of-the-art conformer architecture that combines local CNN feature extraction with transformer self-attention for large-vocabulary ACR, achieving WCSR Root of 84.69\% and MajMin of 84.09\%. While ChordFormer represents the current state-of-the-art for large-vocabulary offline chord recognition, its architectural complexity and reliance on extended temporal context make it less directly applicable to the segment-based, fixed-vocabulary classification scenario studied here. Kusaka and Maezawa \cite{Kusaka2024} proposed Mobile-AMT, achieving an AMT F1-score of 96.30\% with 82.9\% compute reduction, while Hu et al. \cite{Hu2025} adapted Mobile-AMT for strictly causal online operation with latency below 30 ms, confirming that CNN-based architectures can achieve state-of-the-art accuracy even under strict processing constraints.

In the domain of comparative machine learning evaluation, Cui \cite{Cui2022} applied an SVM with Pitch Class Profile (PCP) features for chord recognition, while Putra and Sanjaya \cite{Putra2025} employed SVM with PCP features for automatic chord recognition on guitar and piano, both achieving high accuracy under within-distribution evaluation. These studies, however, do not address the behavior of SVM under different dataset or environment shifts, which is a critical gap that the present paper addresses. The known sensitivity of kernel-based methods to feature distribution changes is well-documented in the broader machine learning literature \cite{Zaman2023} \cite{Zaman2023}: RBF-SVM decision boundaries are implicitly defined relative to the training support vectors, and when the feature distribution shifts (e.g., due to a different instrument's timbre or a different recording device's frequency response), the kernel similarity scores between test samples and support vectors may change drastically, causing systematic misclassification.

Ferdiawan and Hartono \cite{Ferdiawan2022} applied CNN for piano chord sound detection and confirmed the general efficacy of CNN for chord classification from audio spectrograms. Joysingh et al. \cite{Joysingh2022} demonstrated that spectral-domain onset detection algorithms remain competitive for instruments with fast attack characteristics such as piano. Majchrzak and Madziuk \cite{Majchrzak2025} trained chord recognition models on artificially generated audio and evaluated generalization to real recordings, directly addressing the domain shift problem in ACR, a study closely related in spirit to the cross-dataset evaluation in this paper. Their findings confirm that the gap between training dataset and test set accuracy is a persistent challenge in ACR, which positioned deep feature learning as the better approach than hand-crafted or kernel-based features.

The literature review reveals that while CNN-CQT systems have been widely validated for chord classification within controlled dataset conditions, direct head-to-head comparisons with tuned SVM classifiers under cross-dataset evaluation remain underexplored, specifically assessing whether learned representations generalize when acoustic domain conditions change. The present study fills this gap with a controlled experiment using distinct recording hardware, instruments, and environments for training and evaluation.

The application connects to a keyboard via USB MIDI, programmatically triggers chords by sending MIDI note events of each chord, and captures the resulting audio through a microphone. This approach ensures deterministic chord triggering and minimizes occurrences of human error. without manual playing errors and produces recordings with organic acoustic properties arising from the instrument's physical resonance.

\subsubsection{Dataset Specification}\label{subsubsec3_1_2}

The chord vocabulary covers 36 classes organized as the combination of 12 chromatic root notes (C, C$\sharp$, D, D$\sharp$, E, F, F$\sharp$, G, G$\sharp$, A, A$\sharp$, B) and 3 chord quality types (major, minor, diminished). All chords are in the fourth octave, which provides the clearest harmonic separability and optimal spectral overlap with the CQT frequency range \cite{Telila2023} \cite{Harte2010}. The diminished chord type was included to test the classifier's sensitivity to a harmonically dense and less commonly occurring chord type \cite{Aidinis2024}.

\subsubsection{Dataset Categories}\label{subsubsec3_1_3}

The dataset is divided into two categories, each for their own respective uses:

\begin{enumerate}[1.]

    \item Training dataset: 200 samples per class with randomized note attack delays of 0-150 ms, totaling 7,200 samples. The dataset is recorded on Xiaomi Pad 2 and played by Techno T-9890i keyboard with Acoustic Grand Piano voice.

    \item Evaluation dataset: 40 samples per class with randomized note attack delays of 0-100 ms, totaling 1,440 samples. The other 20 half of the samples are recorded with relatively higher volume. The dataset is recorded on ROG Flow X13 and played by Yamaha PSR-S910 keyboard with Grand Piano voice.

\end{enumerate}

Variation in note attack timing simulates natural playing conditions and reduces the risk of the model overfitting to perfectly simultaneous note onsets. All recordings are stored in OGG/OPUS format at 48,000 Hz sample rate, 32-bit depth, mono channel, without any post-processing filters that could alter spectral characteristics. \subsection{Constant-Q Transform}\label{subsec3_3}

Feature extraction uses the Constant-Q Transform (CQT) via the \texttt{librosa.cqt()} function. The CQT computes a time-frequency spectrogram representation in which the center frequency of each bin $f_k$ satisfies a constant ratio $Q = \frac{f_k}{\Delta f_k}$, given by:

\begin{equation}
Q = \frac{f_k}{f_{k+1} - f_k} = \frac{1}{2^{1/B} - 1}
\end{equation}

where $B$ is the number of bins per octave \cite{Li2021} \cite{Lacoste2007}. This logarithmic frequency spacing directly aligns each bin with a musical note, making the resulting 2D spectrogram matrix invariant to chord transposition and ideal for CNN-based pattern recognition \cite{Li2021} \cite{Telila2023}.

\subsection{Feature Extraction Parameters}\label{subsec3_4}

The extraction parameters are given in Table \ref{tab1}.

\begin{table}[h]

The choice of $36$ bins per octave ($3$ bins per semitone) provides high frequency resolution sufficient to resolve the harmonic interval differences between major ($4 + 3$ semitones), minor ($3 + 4$ semitones), and diminished ($3 + 3$ semitones) triads, consistent with the configurations used by Akram et al. \cite{Akram2025}, O'Hanlon and Sandler \cite{OHanlon2021}, and Rowe and Tzanetakis \cite{Akram2025}.

Each audio sample is analyzed to determine the minimum recording duration across the dataset, as shown in Fig. X. Since broken chords with random note delays may be up to $2.7$ seconds long while simultaneous chords are approximately $2.0$ seconds. The frame size is calculated using the following formula:

\begin{equation}
\textit{frame} = \frac{\textit{duration} \times \textit{sample rate}}{\textit{hop size}}
\end{equation}

Corresponding to the minimum audio duration of $2.0$ seconds, the fixed frame length is set to $188$ frames and longer recordings are truncated to this length. This ensures all feature matrices have the uniform dimensionality of $216 \times 188 \times 1$ required by the CNN input layer.

The output of the CQT extraction is a 2D matrix for each audio sample where rows represent frequency bins ($0$--$215$, corresponding to C1--C7) and columns represent time frames ($0$--$187$, spanning $0$--$2$ seconds). Values in each cell represent the magnitude of the CQT coefficient in decibels (dB), capturing the energy distribution across the musical pitch and time dimensions simultaneously.

\subsection{CNN Architecture}\label{subsec3_5}

The CNN architecture is adopted and adapted from \cite{Wolf-Monheim2024} \cite{Wolf-Monheim2025}, originally designed for environmental sound classification using spectral and rhythmic features on the ESC-50 dataset \cite{Purwono2022}. The architecture was selected for its relatively compact form consisting of four convolutional blocks without recurrent layers, attention mechanisms, or secondary models, which aligns with the fixed-window classification approach of this study. This contrasts with architectures such as ChordFormer \cite{Akram2025} or FifthNet-online \cite{Ito2023} that are designed for continuous temporal modeling. The complete layer-by-layer architecture is specified in Table \ref{tab2}.

\begin{table*}[h]
\caption{Proposed CNN architecture specification}\label{tab2}
\begin{tabular*}{\textwidth}{@{\extracolsep\fill}lll}
\toprule
Layer & Output Shape & Kernel / Parameters \\
\midrule
BatchNormalization  & $216 \times 188 \times 1$  & - \\
Conv2D              & $216 \times 188 \times 64$ & $3 \times 3$, ReLU, same padding \\
MaxPooling2D        & $108 \times 94 \times 64$  & $2 \times 2$, stride $2$ \\
Conv2D              & $108 \times 94 \times 128$ & $3 \times 3$, ReLU, same padding \\
MaxPooling2D        & $54 \times 47 \times 128$  & $2 \times 2$, stride $2$ \\
Conv2D              & $54 \times 47 \times 256$  & $3 \times 3$, ReLU, same padding \\
MaxPooling2D        & $27 \times 23 \times 256$  & $2 \times 2$, stride $2$ \\
Conv2D              & $27 \times 23 \times 256$  & $3 \times 3$, ReLU, same padding \\
MaxPooling2D        & $13 \times 11 \times 256$  & $2 \times 2$, stride $2$ \\
Flatten             & $36,608$                   & - \\
Dense               & $256$                      & $256$ neurons, ReLU \\
Dropout             & $256$                      & rate $0.5$ \\
Dense (Output)      & $36$                       & Softmax \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular*}
\end{table*}

The Batch Normalization layer at the input normalizes the CQT magnitude values along the frequency axis, ensuring consistent input statistics across samples and accelerating training convergence \cite{Purwono2022}.

The four Conv2D-MaxPooling2D blocks form a progressive feature hierarchy: the first block ($64$ filters) learns low-level spectral edges and texture features; the second block ($128$ filters) captures mid-level harmonic interval patterns; the third and fourth blocks ($256$ filters each) acquire high-level chord quality representations. The ReLU activation function is used throughout the convolutional and dense layers to introduce nonlinearity while maintaining efficient gradient flow \cite{OShea2015} \cite{Purwono2022}.

MaxPooling with a $2 \times 2$ kernel and stride $2$ at each block reduces the spatial resolution by half, concentrating the learned representation while reducing the parameter count and providing limited translation invariance within the time-frequency plane \cite{Purwono2022}.

After the fourth pooling block, the feature map ($13 \times 11 \times 256$) is flattened to a $36,608$-dimensional vector and passed through a Dense layer with $256$ ReLU neurons for classification learning. A Dropout layer (rate $= 0.5$) randomly deactivates $50\%$ of neurons during training iterations to prevent co-adaptation of neurons and reduce overfitting \cite{Purwono2022}. The output Dense layer uses Softmax activation to produce a probability distribution over the $36$ chord classes, enabling the model to output both a classification decision and a confidence score \cite{Hossin2015}.

Two regularization callbacks are applied during training. \subsection{Cross Validation Method}\label{subsec3_7}

To validate the statistical robustness of the classification results and rule out variance from a single train/test split, 5-fold cross-validation is performed on the full training dataset. In each fold, $80\%$ of the data is used for training and $20\%$ for validation/test. Each fold involves a complete re-training of the model from initialization with fixed random seed per fold, ensuring that performance is measured across five independent data splits.

\subsection{SVM Hyperparameter Tuning}\label{subsec3_8}

An RBF-kernel SVM is configured as a comparative baseline using the scikit-learn SVC implementation \cite{Pedregosa2011}. The identical CQT features used for CNN training are used as SVM input, ensuring a fair comparison. Because SVM operates on flattened feature vectors, each $216 \times 188$ CQT matrix is reshaped into a $40,608$-dimensional vector before classification.

Hyperparameter optimization is performed using the Optuna library \cite{Akiba2019} with RandomSampler over 10 trials. The search space is defined as follows: $C \in \{0.1, 1, 10, 100, 1000\}$ and $\gamma \in \{10^{-6}, 10^{-5}, 10^{-4}, 10^{-3}\}$, following the ranges recommended by Chang and Lin \cite{Chang2011} and consistent with related ACR studies \cite{Putra2025} \cite{Zaman2023}. Each trial is evaluated using stratified 5-fold cross-validation accuracy.

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}\label{subsec3_9}

Model performance is evaluated using the standard classification metrics derived from the confusion matrix \cite{Hossin2015}, where the calculations are based on what is seen in Tables \ref{tab1}.

\begin{table}[h]
\caption{Confusion matrix calculation for evaluation metrics}\label{tab4}
\begin{tabular}{@{}lll@{}}
\toprule
                    & Actual Positive       & Actual Negative \\
\midrule
Predicted Positive  & True Positive (TP)    & False Negative (FN) \\
Predicted Negative  & False Positive (FP)   & True Negative (TN) \\
\botrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

The formula for each metrics are as follows:

\begin{enumerate}[1.]

    \item Accuracy: the proportion of correctly classified samples. Measures the ratio of correct predictions over the total number of instances evaluated.

    \begin{equation}
    \textit{Accuracy}=\frac{TP+TN}{TP+FP+TN+FN} \label{eq1}
    \end{equation}

    \item Precision: the proportion of positive predictions that are correct. Measures the positive patterns that are correctly predicted from the total predicted patterns in a positive class.

    \begin{equation}
    \textit{Precision}=\frac{TP}{TP+FP} \label{eq2}
    \end{equation}

    \item Recall: the proportion of true positives correctly identified. Measures the fraction of positive patterns that are correctly classified.

    \begin{equation}
    \textit{Recall}=\frac{TP}{TP+TN} \label{eq3}
    \end{equation}

    \item F1-score: represents the harmonic mean of precision and recall values.

    \begin{equation}
    \textit{F1-score}=\frac{2\times\textit{Precision}\times\textit{}{Recall}}{\textit{Precision}+\textit{Recall}} \label{eq4}
    \end{equation}

\end{enumerate}

For multi-class classification with 36 classes, macro-averaged precision, recall, and F1-score are reported, giving equal weight to each class regardless of sample count. All metrics are computed from the confusion matrix over the test sets.

On the evaluation dataset, the CNN sustains a high level of performance with $89\%$ accuracy, $91\%$ precision, $89\%$ recall, and $87\%$ F1-score. This demonstrates that the model's learned spectral representations retain substantial discriminative power when applied to a Yamaha PSR-S910 keyboard recorded with a different device in a different environment. The moderate drop from $1.00$ to $0.87$ F1-score reflects the misclassifications concentrated in a small number of harmonically related chord pairs, which are A$\sharp$\_diminished / B\_major, C\_diminished / C$\sharp$\_minor, and E\_diminished / E\_major, as analyzed in Table \ref{tab4_5}, rather than a broad systemic failure across all classes.

This precision-recall asymmetry of $0.64$ vs. $0.29$ is a sign of model collapse. In a $36$-class problem, a classifier predicting a single class for all samples yields a recall of $1/36 \approx 0.028$, which is consistent with the SVM's $0.29$ recall being driven by the small subset of classes it happens to classify correctly. The F1-score of $0.36$ provides the most balanced and informative summary of the SVM's failure, placing it well below the CNN's $0.87$ and confirming that the CNN is the substantially superior model for deployment across diverse instruments and recording environments.

CNN's partial but meaningful generalization is consistent with the model having learned relative spectral interval patterns along the frequency ratios between root, third, and fifth across the CQT bins that are largely preserved regardless of the difference in instruments or recording environments. Compared to the SVM's RBF kernel that measures absolute Euclidean distances in the full $40,608$-dimensional flattened feature space, making it inherently sensitive to any global shift in the spectral envelope between the training and evaluation instruments. This distinction aligns with broader findings in the machine learning literature on covariate shift : kernel classifiers with fixed bandwidths are brittle under distribution shift because the kernel distances between test samples and training support vectors change in unpredictable and uncontrolled ways when the marginal feature distribution shifts.

\section{Conclusion}\label{sec5}

This paper presented the design, training, and cross-dataset evaluation of a CNN model for 36-class piano chord classification using CQT spectrograms, compared against a hyperparameter-tuned RBF-SVM on identical features.

Both models achieve perfect performance on the training dataset with 1.00 accuracy and F1-score across all five cross-validation folds, confirming that CQT spectrograms provide sufficient discriminative information for the task under consistent recording conditions. The decisive finding emerges in cross-dataset evaluation: when tested on a Yamaha PSR-S910 recorded in a different environment from the training data, the CNN retains strong generalization at 89\% accuracy and 87\% macro F1-score, while the SVM collapses to 29\% accuracy and 36\% macro F1-score where it predominantly predicts a single class (C\_diminished\_4) regardless of the true label. This difference directly reflects the structural difference between CNN's spatially local, hierarchical feature learning and SVM's global kernel boundary, which is brittle under different instrument spectral distribution shifts.

The CNN's remaining misclassifications are concentrated in a small number of harmonically related chord pairs of A$\sharp$\_diminished / B\_major, C\_diminished / C$\sharp$\_minor, and E\_diminished / E\_major that share pitch-class content, suggesting that targeted data augmentation across diverse instruments and acoustic environments would be sufficient to close the remaining performance gap. Future work should extend the chord vocabulary beyond fourth-octave triads and integrate this classifier into a low-latency online recognition pipeline for real-time music education applications.
